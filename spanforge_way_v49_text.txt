SYSTEM 01      Core Delivery Framework The SpanForge Exit Gate System™ The Exit Gate System™ is SpanForge’s primary answer to the enterprise AI pilot crisis. It replaces ambiguous, momentum-driven piloting with a formal, evidence-led lifecycle: defined stages, explicit transition criteria, and non-negotiable gate conditions. Every advancement decision is made on documented evidence. Every closure is formally recorded. No initiative advances by momentum alone.  Design Intent The Exit Gate System™ is designed to reduce the likelihood of AI pilot failure by closing the specific governance gaps identified in enterprise AI research. It does not guarantee successful delivery. Outcomes depend on the quality of evidence produced at each gate and the integrity of Gate Authority decisions.  The Five Lifecycle Stages Stage Name Objective Exit Gate Condition 01 Scoping Define the problem, value hypothesis, and data prerequisites Signed problem statement; confirmed data access; sponsor commitment letter 02 Proof of Concept Validate technical feasibility with representative data Baseline accuracy met on holdout set; risk register reviewed; PoC report approved 03 Pilot Validate business value in a controlled production environment KPI targets achieved; operational integration confirmed; compliance sign-off obtained 04 Limited Release Confirm scalability and adoption with real users Adoption rate thresholds met; SLA compliance demonstrated; support model defined 05 Full Production Operate at scale with monitoring and governance Runbook approved; monitoring live; owner accountabilities documented  The Gate Readiness Score™ Before each gate review, the programme team produces a Gate Readiness Score™ — a structured 0–100 assessment across six dimensions.  Dimension What Is Assessed Technical Readiness Model performance, integration completeness, and infrastructure stability Business Value Evidence KPIs defined, measured, and validated against baseline Operational Integration Support model, runbooks, escalation paths, and team training in place Compliance Status Regulatory requirements mapped; data handling compliant; sign-offs obtained Stakeholder Alignment Gate Authority constituted; business sponsor active; team capacity confirmed Cost Readiness (CostGuard™) CostGuard™ cost estimate produced for the selected infrastructure configuration; scenario comparison documented; projected cost within the approved business case envelope  A score below 70 triggers an automatic remediation plan — no gate review is scheduled until the threshold is met. The score replaces narrative presentations with structured, measurable evidence briefs. It gives Gate Authority panels a consistent brief across all initiatives in a portfolio. How the Gate Readiness Score™ Is Calculated Each of the six dimensions is scored from 0 to 100 by the initiative team, then independently reviewed by the Gate Authority. The overall GRS is the weighted average across all six dimensions. The weighting is fixed per gate stage — earlier gates weight Stakeholder Alignment and Business Value Evidence more heavily (each at 25% of the total score); later gates shift weight toward Operational Integration and Compliance Status (each at 25%) as the initiative approaches production. Cost Readiness is weighted equally at 10% across all gates where a Design phase has been completed. A dimension score below 50 is an automatic flag regardless of overall score — the Gate Authority must address the flagged dimension explicitly before an Advance decision is recorded. A dimension cannot be averaged away. This prevents teams from gaming a high overall score by excelling on four dimensions while neglecting one critical area. Who scores it:  The initiative team produces a scored brief at least five business days before the scheduled gate review. The Gate Authority reviews and may adjust scores with documented rationale. Any adjustment of more than 20 points on any dimension must be explained in the gate record. The final accepted score — not the team’s initial score — is the score of record.  The Gate Decision Framework Decision Meaning What Happens Next Advance All gate conditions satisfied with documented evidence Moves to next stage on a confirmed schedule Conditional Advance Conditions substantially met; time-bounded remediation commitments defined Advances with a named review trigger and accountability owner Return Conditions not met — a designed outcome, not a failure Returns to current stage with documented rationale, remediation period, and captured learnings  Important A gate that every initiative passes is not a governance mechanism. It is a rubber stamp with extra steps. If gate reviews never result in a Return decision, evidence thresholds are too low.  The SpanForge Pilot Risk Index™ — What Each Gate Is Designed to Prevent Gate Failure Mode Addressed Mechanism Gate 1 — Scoping Wrong problem defined Forces a written, signed problem statement before any technical work begins Gate 2 — PoC Technical infeasibility undetected Requires demonstrated accuracy on representative data before pilot investment Gate 3 — Pilot No measurable business value Mandates KPI validation in a real operating environment, not a sandbox Gate 4 — Limited Release No user adoption Requires measured adoption rates and SLA compliance from actual users before full deployment Gate 5 — Production No scalability or governance Confirms runbooks, monitoring, and owner accountability before removing programme controls Pilot Risk Index™ Scoring The Pilot Risk Index™ (PRI) score is calculated at each gate review by assessing which failure modes remain unaddressed. Each unaddressed failure mode contributes points to the aggregate score. A PRI score of zero means all gate conditions are satisfied. A score above 40 at any gate triggers a mandatory Return decision. The table below shows the point contribution of each failure mode when it remains unaddressed at the time of the gate review. Each failure mode is assessed at the gate where it is most relevant — the theoretical maximum of 100 points is not achievable at any single gate, as Gate 1 failure modes cannot be assessed at Gate 5 and vice versa. The practical maximum at any gate is typically 40–60 points. Without this, gate decisions become advisory rather than binding. A budget-accountable business sponsor is required at every gate — not a delegate. The Gate Authority applies the three-outcome decision framework: Advance, Conditional Advance, or Return. Undocumented Return decisions are a system failure — they eliminate the audit trail and create ambiguity about what the team must produce next.  Scope: When NOT to Apply the Exit Gate System™ The Exit Gate System™ is designed for AI initiatives with material business impact, production integration requirements, and meaningful stakeholder accountability. Three categories fall outside its intended scope.  Initiative Type Recommended Alternative Low-risk internal automation — scripted or rules-based, limited blast radius Lightweight two-gate model Exploratory R&D and horizon-scanning — objective is learning, not deployment Time and budget boundary model only Short technical spikes (under six weeks) — answering a specific feasibility question Input to Gate 1 scoping decision; not a pilot programme  The test: if an initiative could cause material disruption in production, affects customers or regulated data, or requires significant cross-functional coordination — it belongs inside the Exit Gate System™. Every advancement requires documented evidence; advancement by momentum is the system's primary failure mode. Returning an initiative to a prior stage is a designed outcome, not a failure signal. The system is not appropriate for every AI experiment — calibrate governance to risk level.   SYSTEM 02      Governance Framework The T.R.U.S.T. Framework™ The T.R.U.S.T. Framework is SpanForge's governance layer. It converts AI ethics from policy documents into technical controls, compliance documentation, and audit-ready evidence. Every SpanForge-certified AI system is designed to satisfy all five dimensions. SpanForge certification means the system has completed the full five-phase lifecycle, passed all six Build gates, satisfied every T.R.U.S.T. dimension in the Govern phase, and has SpanForge active in production. Certification is self-attested by the named Gate Authority sponsor and supported by the complete audit trail of gate records, risk registers, and incident playbooks.  T.R.U.S.T. Definition: Authoritative Standard The authoritative T.R.U.S.T. definition is: Transparency, Responsibility, User Rights, Safety Guardrails, Traceability. This White Paper version provides operationally specific governance dimensions and is the standard used across all platform documentation. The public website will be updated to reflect this definition.  The Five Dimensions Letter Dimension What It Means T Transparency Customers, regulators, and employees understand how AI affects them. AI behaviour is made intelligible to all affected parties — not just technical teams. Decision driver visualisation surfaces the top contributing factors to each AI decision in business-readable terms. R Responsibility A named human is accountable for every AI system. AI cannot be deployed without a designated owner who carries accountability for its behaviour in production. Responsibility extends to cost: CostGuard™ makes infrastructure cost visible at Design time, ensuring that accountable owners understand the financial implications of their architecture decisions before committing to them. U User Rights Consent, transparency, and recourse for every individual AI affects. Users have the right to understand how AI decisions affect them and to seek redress where required. S Safety Guardrails Technical constraints embedded in architecture, not just policy. Safety mechanisms are built into the system — not left as aspirational guidance or documents. T Traceability Every AI decision must be traceable to its source data, model version, and configuration state. Full audit trail. No black boxes. Every decision is logged with an immutable, timestamped, cryptographically signed record — ready for regulators, auditors, and post-incident review.  How the T.R.U.S.T. Framework Is Operationalised The T.R.U.S.T. Framework is embedded across three platform phases: Design phase (CostGuard™) — cost accountability embedded via the Cost Readiness dimension of the Gate Readiness Score™. Responsible owners must understand and document the financial implications of architecture decisions before any resource is committed. Govern phase — compliance mapping (including EU AI Act), risk registers, governance maturity assessment, and board-level reporting, all structured around the five dimensions. Scale phase (SpanForge) — technical enforcement of Traceability (audit trail), User Rights (consent boundary monitoring), and Safety Guardrails (automated playbooks) in production.  Key Takeaways The T.R.U.S.T. Framework converts governance intent into technical controls — not just documented policies. Responsibility, Traceability, and cost accountability are enforced technically — via a pre-commitment cost brief at Design time (Cost Readiness dimension, automated by CostGuard when live) and via SpanForge in production. Neither is left to human process alone. Compliance documentation produced through the Govern phase is designed to withstand EU AI Act regulatory scrutiny. Under the EU AI Act (in force from August 2026), AI systems are classified as minimal, limited, high, or unacceptable risk — high-risk systems (those affecting safety, employment, credit, education, or public services) require conformity assessments, technical documentation, and mandatory human oversight before deployment. The Framework applies to every AI system in the SpanForge lifecycle — it is not optional at any gate. Phase 02 — Design The Design phase translates a validated problem into a concrete technical architecture. It covers every major decision that must be made before production code is written. Model selection — evaluate candidate models against use-case requirements, cost constraints, and regulatory position Data strategy — define ingestion pipelines, preprocessing, feature engineering, and versioning approach Infrastructure decisions — select compute, storage, and serving architecture Integration patterns — define API contracts, fallback strategies, and downstream dependencies The Design phase includes tooling that benchmarks local LLM infrastructure and produces a model readiness score — so teams know precisely what they can run before committing to an architecture. CostGuard™ — Decision-Time Cost Intelligence The Design phase has a documented gap: it governs which model to select and which infrastructure to deploy, but has not previously governed the cost of those decisions at the moment they are made. CostGuard™ closes this gap. It is a pre-deployment cost intelligence tool built natively into the SpanForge Design phase, ensuring that every architecture decision carries a cost estimate before any resource is committed. CostGuard™ operates at the decision point — not after it. Just as the model readiness score tells teams what they can run, CostGuard™ tells them what it will cost to run it. The two outputs together constitute a complete Design phase decision brief: capability and cost, both known before a single line of production code is written. Core CostGuard™ capabilities: •  Pre-commitment cost prediction — infrastructure config or model selection produces a cost estimate before any resource is created •  Scenario comparison — cheapest, balanced, and performance architectures evaluated side by side at Design time •  Optimisation recommendations — actionable cost-saving changes ranked by impact, aligned to Gate Readiness Score™ evidence requirements •  Continuous cost feedback loop — actual vs. predicted costs feed back into the Design phase, improving future estimate accuracy across the portfolio CostGuard™ is the SpanForge answer to the question the market cannot currently answer at design time: what will this architecture cost before we build it? How CostGuard™ integrates: When CostGuard is live, it will accept Terraform configurations, infrastructure YAML, and direct CLI inputs, connecting to cloud pricing APIs to produce real-time estimates. The output will be a structured cost brief: a baseline estimate, three scenario comparisons (cheapest, balanced, performance), and a ranked list of optimisation actions. This brief feeds into the Design Exit Gate evidence package and the Gate Readiness Score™ Cost Readiness dimension. Until CostGuard ships, the structured manual cost brief template satisfies this requirement.  CostGuard™ Capability Roadmap The following table reflects CostGuard™ capability status as of April 2026. Human-in-the-Loop System Design Human oversight is not simply a feature — it is a system design decision that must be made explicitly at the architecture stage and enforced technically in production. SpanForge treats human-in-the-loop (HITL) as a first-class system component.  HITL Design Dimension Design Decision Required When humans must intervene Define confidence thresholds per decision type. Any output below threshold is automatically queued for human review before reaching users or downstream systems. Cost vs. accuracy tradeoff Low thresholds increase human review volume and cost. High thresholds increase autonomous decision risk. This tradeoff must be explicitly modelled and approved at Gate 3. Approval flows Who reviews queued decisions? What is the SLA for review? What happens if a review times out? These flows must be defined, tested, and documented before Limited Release. Audit of human decisions Human review decisions are logged with the same immutability as automated decisions. This is required for regulatory compliance and post-incident analysis. Review interface design The interface presented to human reviewers must provide sufficient context — without creating cognitive overload — to support a reliable, accountable decision. SYSTEM 04      SpanForge Platform SpanForge You cannot govern what you cannot see. SpanForge is the production observability and governance platform for autonomous AI agents. It provides behavioural baselining, drift detection, consent boundary enforcement, and automated response playbooks — before regulators, users, or incident reports find the problem. CostGuard, SpanForge’s cost intelligence capability, is currently in development; the Design phase cost requirement is satisfied in the interim through a structured manual cost brief process. Traditional observability tells you if systems are running. SpanForge tells you if they are behaving correctly. The 10 SpanForge Observability Namespaces Every SpanForge event is emitted into one of ten namespaces defined by RFC-0001 SPANFORGE. The namespaces cover the complete observability surface of an autonomous AI agent — from individual decisions to the immutable audit chain. The SDK instruments all ten namespaces by default. Individual namespaces can be configured independently per deployment context.  # Namespace What It Observes 01 decision Every individual agent decision: input, output, confidence score, latency, and decision type 02 tool_call All external tool invocations: API calls, database queries, code execution, file reads — with inputs, outputs, and latency 03 chain Multi-step prompt chain execution: step sequence, inter-step data flow, cumulative error propagation 04 confidence Output confidence score distributions: per decision type, per model, tracked against baseline 05 consent Data access decisions: which fields, sources, and APIs were accessed, against the approved consent boundary 06 drift Statistical drift signals: Z-score and KL-divergence against deployment baseline, per metric and per time window 07 latency End-to-end response time, per-step latency breakdown, SLA compliance tracking 08 hitl Human-in-the-loop events: queue entries, review decisions, SLA timers, timeout actions, and reviewer identity 09 playbook Automated playbook executions: trigger event, action taken, outcome, and HMAC-signed timestamp 10 audit Immutable audit chain: every event cross-referenced with HMAC signature for tamper-evident regulatory export   Core Capabilities Capability Technical Detail Behavioural Baselining Instruments the agent at first deployment. However, as SpanForge matures, engineering, sales, and delivery teams need to know which elements are buildable today, which are governance frameworks, and which are analytical models.  Every construct in this document is tagged in the System Hierarchy table (Section 8.1). The tags are:  Tag What It Means for Engineering / Delivery [SYSTEM] A non-negotiable governance or delivery mechanism. Enforced on every engagement without exception. [PRODUCT] A buildable software component. Has a live version or active development roadmap. Described by technical specifications. [MODEL] An analytical or diagnostic tool. Applied with judgement. Not enforced — used to understand and communicate. [TOOL] A required artefact, template, or structured document. Produced as part of the delivery process. [CONCEPTUAL] A principle, belief, or doctrine. Informs culture and communication. Not a delivery mechanism and not enforced.  Engineering note Only [PRODUCT] items have technical specifications and development roadmaps. [SYSTEM] items define operational requirements that products must satisfy. Never build a [MODEL] as if it were a [PRODUCT] — they serve different purposes. 8.5 — Specification Governance This document is version-controlled. Every change must be approved and documented. Without discipline here, the SpanForge Way becomes a living document in the worst sense — informal, inconsistent, and impossible to train against.  Versioning rules: Major version (e.g. 4.0 → 5.0): New system added, existing system fundamentally redesigned, or a Non-Negotiable changed. Requires founding team approval and full team briefing within 5 business days. Minor version (e.g. 4.5 → 4.6): New section added, existing section significantly expanded, or a scoring methodology changed. Requires approval from at least two founding team members. Patch version (e.g. 4.5 → 4.5.1): Typo corrections, clarifications that do not change meaning, formatting fixes. One founding team member can approve. No undocumented changes: Every change must be logged in the changelog (below) with the date, author, and description. Undocumented changes are not valid.  Backward compatibility: Gate exit criteria are backward compatible within a major version. An initiative that entered at Gate 1 under v4.x will not have its gate criteria changed mid-lifecycle. Scoring methodology changes (GRS weights, PRI scores) take effect on new initiatives only — not retroactively on active initiatives. Product capability changes (CostGuard™ roadmap, SpanForge namespaces) are updated in patch versions and do not require a minor version bump.  Deprecation policy: A construct is deprecated when it is superseded by a more precise replacement. Deprecated constructs are marked [DEPRECATED] in the System Hierarchy table and remain for two major versions before removal. No construct is silently removed. Every deprecation is announced to the full team with a transition period of at least 90 days.
